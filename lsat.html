The approach

Diagnostic first.
Everything else follows.

Most LSAT tutoring starts in the wrong place. Students are given drills before anyone has identified what's actually holding their score back. Hours are spent reinforcing habits rather than replacing them.

My approach starts with a rigorous diagnostic — understanding not just what questions a student gets wrong, but why, and what the pattern of errors reveals about the underlying reasoning habits at play. That diagnosis shapes everything that follows.

My own score of 176 is not a marketing detail. It is the foundation of what I understand about the test that most instructors never reach — the difference between knowing how to solve a problem and knowing why the problem is constructed the way it is. That distinction changes the quality of the instruction.

The LSAT rewards a specific kind of disciplined, analytical thinking. That thinking can be taught. Students who believed they had reached their ceiling have consistently found, with the right instruction, that they had not.
